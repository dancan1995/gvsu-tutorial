Descriptive Statistics

Steps in SPSS:

  1. Go to Analyze > Descriptive Statistics > Frequencies.
  2. Select the variables you want to summarize (e.g., age, weight, gender).
  3. Click OK to generate frequencies or descriptive statistics.

Example Output:

Variable Interpretations

Age: The sample's age distribution has a mean of 48 years and a standard deviation of 7.97 years, indicating moderate spread. Most individuals fall between 35 (minimum) and 63 (maximum), with the most frequent age being 35.

BMI: The average BMI is 29.07, indicating participants are mostly in the overweight range. The values vary narrowly, with a standard deviation of 2.38, spanning from 24.3 to 33.0. The most frequent BMI is 28.5.

SBP_Pre: The mean SBP before intervention is 144.3 mmHg, with a standard deviation of 8.07 mmHg. Values range from 130 to 155 mmHg, indicating a hypertensive group requiring intervention. The most common SBP is 145 mmHg.

DBP_Pre: The mean DBP is 93 mmHg, with a standard deviation of 7.23 mmHg. Values range from 80 to 105 mmHg, with 95 mmHg being the most frequent value, indicating high diastolic pressure.

SBP_Post: The mean SBP decreased to 135.2 mmHg, with a narrower standard deviation of 6.21 mmHg. Values now range from 125 to 145 mmHg, with 135 mmHg as the most frequent value, reflecting improved control post-intervention.

DBP_Post: The mean DBP decreased to 87.3 mmHg, with a standard deviation of 4.74 mmHg. Values range from 78 to 95 mmHg, with the most frequent value being 85 mmHg, indicating tighter control of diastolic pressure.

Conclusion

The analysis of the sample's health metrics reveals several critical insights regarding the participants' demographics and health status. The age distribution indicates a mature population, predominantly in the overweight category, as reflected by the average BMI. The elevated systolic and diastolic blood pressure readings prior to intervention suggest a significant prevalence of hypertension among participants, highlighting a need for medical intervention.

Post-intervention results demonstrate a marked improvement in both systolic and diastolic blood pressure, indicating that the intervention was effective in managing hypertension. This positive change suggests that the strategies employed during the intervention were successful in promoting better cardiovascular health among participants.

In conclusion, the data underscores the importance of targeted health interventions for managing weight and blood pressure, particularly in populations at risk due to age and existing health conditions. Continued monitoring and further studies may be beneficial to assess long-term outcomes and refine intervention strategies.

Independent Samples T-Test

Steps in SPSS:

  1. Go to Analyze > Compare Means > Independent-Samples T-Test.
  2. Select your test variable (e.g., systolic blood pressure) and grouping variable (e.g., gender).
  3. Define the groups (e.g., 1 = Male, 2 = Female).
  4. Click OK to run the test.

Example Output:

Independent Samples Test Interpretation

Analysis of SBP_Pre

The mean systolic blood pressure (SBP) for males was 147.00 mmHg (SD = 6.851), while for females, it was 141.60 mmHg (SD = 8.345). Levene's test for equality of variances yielded an F-value of 1.629 (p = 0.205), indicating equal variances can be assumed. The independent samples t-test revealed a statistically significant difference in SBP between genders, with t(98) = 3.537, p = 0.001. The mean difference of 5.400 mmHg (SE = 1.527) indicates that males had a higher SBP than females. The 95% confidence interval for this difference ranged from 2.370 to 8.430 mmHg.

Analysis of DBP_Pre

The mean diastolic blood pressure (DBP) for males was 94.00 mmHg (SD = 5.890), while for females, it was 92.00 mmHg (SD = 8.305). Levene's test for equality of variances yielded an F-value of 2.100 (p = 0.150), supporting the assumption of equal variances. The independent samples t-test showed no statistically significant difference in DBP between genders, with t(98) = 1.389, p = 0.168. The mean difference was 2.000 mmHg (SE = 1.440), with a 95% confidence interval of -0.858 to 4.858 mmHg.

Conclusion

The results indicate a significant difference in SBP between males and females, with males having higher SBP. However, no significant difference was observed in DBP between genders. Further investigation is warranted to understand the factors contributing to these differences.

Paired Samples T-Test

Steps in SPSS:

  1. Go to Analyze > Compare Means > Paired-Samples T-Test.
  2. Select the pair of variables to compare (e.g., pre- and post-intervention blood pressure).
  3. Click OK to run the test.

Example Output:

Interpretation: The paired samples statistics compare diastolic blood pressure (DBP) before (Pre) and after (Post) intervention in 100 participants. The mean DBP decreased significantly from 93.00 to 87.30, with a high correlation (r = .890, p < .001), indicating consistency in the paired measurements. The paired samples t-test reveals a mean difference of 5.70 (95% CI: 4.96 to 6.44), showing a statistically significant reduction in DBP (t = 15.373, df = 99, p < .001). This suggests the intervention effectively reduced DBP. The small standard error (.371) and significant results highlight the reliability and importance of this finding.

One-Way ANOVA

Steps in SPSS:

  1. Go to Analyze > Compare Means > One-Way ANOVA.
  2. Select your dependent variable (e.g., systolic blood pressure).
  3. Select your factor (grouping) variable (e.g., age group: young, middle-aged, older).
  4. Click OK to run the test.

Example Output:

Hypotheses

Null Hypothesis (H0): There is no significant difference in the pre-intervention systolic blood pressure (SBP_Pre) between groups defined by compliance levels.

Alternative Hypothesis (H1): There is a significant difference in the pre-intervention systolic blood pressure (SBP_Pre) between groups defined by compliance levels.

Interpretation

The one-way ANOVA test evaluates SBP_Pre differences across 8 compliance groups. The results indicate a statistically significant difference in SBP_Pre among the groups (F = 325.470, p < .001). The between-groups variability (Sum of Squares = 6191.000) is much larger than the within-groups variability (Sum of Squares = 250.000), suggesting that compliance groups account for substantial variance in SBP_Pre. The mean square for between groups (884.429) far exceeds that within groups (2.717), reflecting strong group effects.

Conclusion

Since the p-value is less than .05, we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that compliance levels significantly influence pre-intervention systolic blood pressure. Further post hoc tests are recommended to identify specific group differences.

Chi-Square Test

Steps in SPSS:

  1. Go to Analyze > Descriptive Statistics > Crosstabs.
  2. Place the categorical variable in the Row(s) and Column(s) fields.
  3. Click on Statistics and check Chi-square.
  4. Click OK to run the test.

Example Output:

Crosstabulation and Chi-Square Test Results

Interpretation: The crosstabulation results highlight significant differences in outcomes between genders. Among females, 80% (40 out of 50) showed improvement, while 20% (10 out of 50) showed partial improvement. In contrast, all males (100%) demonstrated improvement, with no instances of partial improvement. Females accounted for 44.4% of the "Improved" group and 100% of the "Partially Improved" group, whereas males contributed 55.6% to the "Improved" group and 0% to the "Partially Improved" group. Overall, 90% of participants improved, and 10% had partial improvement.

The Chi-Square test further confirms a significant association between gender and outcomes. The Pearson Chi-Square statistic (11.111, p = .001) and other tests, including the Fisher’s Exact Test (p = .001), indicate strong evidence of gender differences in outcomes. Notably, all expected cell counts exceed 5, ensuring the test's validity. These findings suggest gender-specific patterns, with males showing consistently better outcomes and females exhibiting variability, warranting further investigation into underlying factors.

Correlation Analysis

Steps in SPSS:

  1. Go to Analyze > Correlate > Bivariate.
  2. Select the variables you want to analyze (e.g., hours studied and test scores).
  3. Check Pearson and click OK.

Example Output:

Correlation Matrix

Correlation Matrix Interpretation

1. Age and BMI

There is a strong positive correlation between Age and BMI (0.881, p < 0.01), meaning that older individuals tend to have higher BMI values.

2. Age and Blood Pressure (SBP_Pre, DBP_Pre, SBP_Post, DBP_Post)

Age is positively correlated with all blood pressure variables, with the correlations ranging from 0.592 (SBP_Pre) to 0.626 (SBP_Post). All these correlations are statistically significant (p < 0.01), suggesting that older individuals tend to have higher blood pressure values before and after measurement.

3. BMI and Blood Pressure (SBP_Pre, DBP_Pre, SBP_Post, DBP_Post)

BMI also shows positive correlations with all blood pressure variables, ranging from 0.505 (DBP_Post) to 0.554 (SBP_Post), all statistically significant (p < 0.01). This implies that higher BMI values are associated with higher blood pressure values.

4. SBP_Pre and DBP_Pre

These two pre-measurements of blood pressure have a very strong positive correlation (0.952, p < 0.01), indicating that people with higher systolic blood pressure before measurement tend to have higher diastolic blood pressure prior to measurement as well.

5. SBP_Post and DBP_Post

Similarly, there is a very strong positive correlation (0.980, p < 0.01) between the post-measurement systolic and diastolic blood pressures, meaning that higher systolic blood pressure after measurement correlates highly with higher diastolic blood pressure after measurement.

6. Pre and Post Blood Pressure Measures

  • SBP_Pre and SBP_Post have a very strong positive correlation (0.933, p < 0.01), meaning that higher systolic blood pressure before measurement is associated with higher systolic blood pressure after measurement.
  • DBP_Pre and DBP_Post also show a very strong positive correlation (0.890, p < 0.01), meaning that higher diastolic blood pressure before measurement is associated with higher diastolic blood pressure after measurement.

7. Compliance

Compliance has weak and non-significant correlations with the other variables:

  • Compliance and Age: A very weak positive correlation of 0.054 (p = 0.596), not statistically significant.
  • Compliance and BMI: A weak positive correlation of 0.133 (p = 0.188), not statistically significant.
  • Compliance and Blood Pressure (Pre and Post): All correlations with blood pressure values (both pre and post) are weak and non-significant, ranging from 0.064 (SBP_Pre) to -0.123 (DBP_Post). This suggests that compliance with the intervention is not strongly associated with age, BMI, or blood pressure levels.

Summary

Age and BMI show strong correlations with each other and with blood pressure measures, both pre and post.

Blood pressure measures (SBP and DBP) are highly correlated with each other across pre- and post-measurements.

Compliance does not show significant relationships with age, BMI, or blood pressure levels, indicating that it may not be influenced by these variables in this dataset.

Kaplan-Meier Survival Analysis

Steps in SPSS:

  1. Go to Analyze > Survival > Kaplan-Meier.
  2. Select the time variable (e.g., survival time) and status variable (e.g., censored or event occurrence).
  3. Define the event status by coding (e.g., 1 for event occurred, 0 for censored).
  4. Under Options, choose to display the survival plot and log-rank test if needed.
  5. Click OK to run the analysis and generate the survival curve.

Example Output:

Survival Table

Time (Months) Number at Risk Number of Events Survival Probability
1 100 2 0.98
3 98 8 0.90
6 90 15 0.75
12 75 20 0.55

Interpretation: The Kaplan-Meier analysis shows that the survival probability decreases over time. After 12 months, the estimated survival probability is 55%, meaning 55% of the subjects are expected to survive past 12 months. The number of events (deaths or other endpoints) increases as time progresses, leading to a decline in survival probability.

Based on the Kaplan-Meier survival curve, the survival rate drops sharply between 6 and 12 months, indicating that this period might be critical in the study population. Further analysis, such as the log-rank test, can be conducted to compare survival curves across different groups (e.g., treatment vs. control).
